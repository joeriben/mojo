### Kernthese
The article examines how elite US college athletes are governed through information asymmetries in data-intensive sports organizations. Based on interviews with athletes and staff, it reveals that athletes in high-status sports (often Black men) have less freedom to see or dispute their personal data, while athletes overall are more comfortable sharing data with distant parties. The authors develop a theory of collective informational harm in bounded institutional settings, proposing the concept of the "quantified organization" that builds data collectives through category creation and managerial relations of coercion and consent.
