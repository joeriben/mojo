### Kernthese
Sharon Todd critiques “strong” instrumentalism in education (mechanistic, economically aligned) and proposes a “weak” instrumentalism through an aesthetic lens, drawing parallels between education and art as fields of practice. Using John Dewey's aesthetics and Nicolas Bourriaud's relational aesthetics, she argues that notions of experience, relation, and formation/transformation are central to understanding educational practice as giving form to specifically educational ideas, illustrated by two projects of the feminist art collective Sisters Hope.
