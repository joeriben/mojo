### Kernthese
The article analyzes how Puerto Rican activists responded to Hurricane Maria by building "alternative sociotechnical infrastructures" – heterogeneous assemblages of actors, organizations, and technologies that bypass state failures. Using a "seamful work" framework, it reveals activists' navigation of colonial and neoliberal state structures, emphasizing bottom-up resilience rather than restoration of existing infrastructure.

The article's analysis of grassroots infrastructure-building as resistance to state failure parallels Jörissen's conceptualization of cultural resilience as collective, relational processes of resistance. Both critique individualistic approaches to resilience and emphasize bottom-up agency. However, the new article focuses specifically on disaster response in colonial contexts, while Jörissen develops cultural resilience as a structural moment for educational theory.

**Jörissen/Klepacki/Klepacki (2025) Nachhaltigkeit und kulturelle Resilienz** (`KGZXSKKC`, parallel)
Both texts engage with resilience as collective, distributed processes rather than individual adaptation. The new article's emphasis on heterogeneous assemblages of actors, organizations, and technologies resonates with Jörissen's analysis of relational becoming in distributed agencies. However, while Jörissen builds on Katrina Brown's framework (rootedness, resourcefulness, resistance) for educational theory, the new article applies a 'seamful work' framework from HCI/STS to colonial infrastructure critique.
