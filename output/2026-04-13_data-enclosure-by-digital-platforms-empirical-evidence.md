### Kernthese
The article presents the first comprehensive empirical study of how digital platforms have used contractual terms to enclose publicly available data over the past decade. Analyzing Terms of Use from 279 platforms in 2012 and 2022, it identifies a systematic increase in both restrictive and permissive clauses, showing that platforms adopted a dual strategy: restricting third-party research access while preserving their own data usage. The study argues that privacy regulation (especially GDPR) has inadvertently expanded platforms' discretion over data access, facilitating rather than curtailing data enclosures.

### Methodisch / theoretisch
The article employs a legal-empirical methodology, conducting a content analysis of 279 platforms' Terms of Use from two time points (2012, 2022) to track changes in contractual clauses regarding data access for research. It draws on contract theory, data governance literature, and platform regulation studies, with a focus on boilerplate contracts and regulatory adaptation.
