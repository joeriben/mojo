### Kernthese
The article critiques the concept of "interdisciplinarity" as inadequate for capturing the full complexity of collaborative research, proposing "pluralistic collaboration" as a more comprehensive alternative. It identifies four dimensions of tension in such collaborations—epistemic, cultural, normative, and work-style—and develops a typology of academic work styles (isolationist, imperialist, pragmatist, pluralist) to analyze individual dynamics sociologically. The central claim is that pluralism across these dimensions can improve problem-solving but risks detrimental effects if over-pluralized.
